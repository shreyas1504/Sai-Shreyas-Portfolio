01
Where This Started

Not a textbook problem.
I watched it happen.

During my SDE internship building the SMARTeIS multi-tenant invoicing platform at Skill Quotient Group, I was in a lot of the same Slack channels as the PM team. And I kept noticing the same pattern play out every week — the PM would spend Monday morning writing the same project context three times: once in a Jira comment, once in a stakeholder email, once in a Slack update to the Eng lead.

Nothing was wrong. The PM was competent. The tools worked fine. But the tools didn't talk to each other, didn't remember context, and couldn't draft anything. Every week started from zero. When I mentioned this to our PM, he said something that stuck with me:

"I'm basically a human ETL pipeline. I ingest status from five places and output the same summary six times a week."

— PM, Skill Quotient Group

That sentence became the seed of this case study. I started asking more questions — what does a PM actually spend time on? What parts add creative value, and what parts are just translation work? Eight user interviews later, I had a much clearer picture of the problem.

💡 Honest disclaimer: This is a portfolio case study, not a shipped product. I conducted real user research, then worked through the full product + architecture decisions as if I were the PM building it. The goal is to show how I think through hard product problems end-to-end.
02
Discovery & Research

What I actually
learned from PMs.

I ran 8 semi-structured interviews over two weeks — 5 PMs I connected with through NYU's MOT program, 2 from my internship context, and 1 cold outreach to a PM at a B2B SaaS company. My goal was to understand where time actually goes, not validate a pre-existing hypothesis.

Research Methodology

// Interview Protocol
Format
30 min Zoom, semi-structured. Started with "walk me through last Tuesday" — open-ended, no leading
Sample
n=8. Mix: 3 mid-level PMs, 2 PM leads, 1 APM, 1 TPM, 1 startup founder-PM. All in B2B SaaS
Analysis
Affinity mapping in FigJam. Clustered pain points, looked for Jobs-to-be-Done language
Bias check
Asked "what's going right with your tools?" to avoid confirmation bias before hitting pain points

Key Findings — What I Didn't Expect

Pain #1 — Most Common

Status communication is the real killer

7 of 8 PMs mentioned this unprompted. Writing the same project status for different audiences (engineering, leadership, sales, customers) took 3–5 hours/week. The frustration wasn't volume — it was that they were writing the same thing over and over with minor reframes.

Pain #2 — Most Nuanced

Context loss is an invisible tax

"Why did we decide X six months ago?" was a question PMs either couldn't answer or spent 20 minutes tracing through Slack and old docs to find. This was especially painful when a new stakeholder joined mid-project and needed to get up to speed fast.

Surprise Finding

PMs don't want AI to replace them — they want a first draft

When I asked "would you want AI to write your status updates automatically?", 6/8 said no — they feared losing their voice. But "would you want AI to draft it and you edit?" got unanimous yes. This reframed my entire product concept.

Opportunity Unlocked

Onboarding is 3–6 weeks of lost productivity

New PMs spend their first month just trying to understand why decisions were made. Nobody documents decisions properly in real time. An AI that could answer "what's the context behind epic #247?" would be immediately valuable to any PM lead.

"We don't need another docs tool. We need something that already knows what we're building and can just help us communicate it."

— PM Lead, B2B Fintech, interview participant

Jobs-to-be-Done Framework

I translated interview themes into JTBD format — this helped me move from "PM is frustrated" to "what is the PM actually trying to accomplish when they do X?" These became my product requirements anchors.

// Core Jobs to be Done
Job 1
When I need to update stakeholders, help me communicate status without starting from scratch, so I don't lose 30 minutes writing the same thing I wrote last week
Job 2
When a stakeholder asks why we made a decision, help me surface the original rationale fast, so I don't look disorganized or spend an hour archaeology-ing through Slack
Job 3
When a new PM joins my team, help them understand our product context in days, not weeks, without interrupting the rest of the team constantly
Job 4
When engineering asks about roadmap priorities, help me answer confidently and consistently, without having to re-explain things that are already documented somewhere
03
Problem Definition

The tools aren't broken.
They're blind.

Jira, Notion, Confluence, and Slack are all good at what they do: tracking, documenting, and communicating. But none of them understand product context. They're static. They don't know your roadmap priorities. They can't synthesize across sources. They can't tell a new PM why a decision was made.

This is a product intelligence problem, not a storage problem. The information exists — it's just trapped in disconnected silos, unsearchable by meaning, and unreachable without human translation. PMs are filling that gap with their own time.

Before — The Reality
  • 4–5 hrs/week writing status updates from scratch for different audiences
  • 20+ minutes tracing a decision through Slack threads and old docs
  • 3–6 weeks for a new PM to get full product context from the team
  • PM becomes the synchronous bottleneck for all stakeholder questions
  • Decision rationale decays — nobody remembers "why" after 3 months
After — The Vision
  • Draft generated in 90 seconds, PM reviews and edits for 5 minutes
  • Ask in plain English: "Why did we defer push notifications?" → instant answer with citations
  • New PMs query the AI on context, onboard 3x faster
  • Stakeholders get a self-serve portal for roadmap questions — no PM required
  • Every decision auto-logged with context, searchable forever

05
System Architecture

How I'd actually
build this.

The co-pilot is a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system that sits on top of your existing product stack. It doesn't replace your tools — it reads them, builds a semantic index of all your product context, and answers queries against that live index with citations. Here's why I made the core architecture decisions I did.

Why RAG, not Fine-tuning?

Fine-tuning — Why I Ruled It Out

Fine-tuning bakes a snapshot of your product context into model weights. When the roadmap changes next sprint, your model is out of date. Retraining is expensive, slow, and means the model's reasoning is invisible — you can't point to the source of an answer. For a trust-critical enterprise product, that's a dealbreaker.

RAG — Why It Fits

RAG retrieves live documents at query time. Always current. Every answer cites a specific source the PM can verify. Org data never leaves your infrastructure — the LLM only sees the relevant chunks retrieved for that query, not all your raw data. Permission-gating happens at retrieval time, not just at the UI layer.

🔐 Permission scoping is the hardest problem here. When one user's AI can potentially surface another user's confidential docs, you don't have a features problem — you have an IAM problem. Retrieval must be permission-gated at the vector store level, not just filtered at the UI after fetching. This was the key architecture constraint I discovered through research.
06
UX Design

Design for
humans reviewing AI,
not AI replacing humans.

My research finding — that PMs want a fast first draft, not AI making decisions for them — became the entire UX principle: the co-pilot always drafts, the PM always approves. This isn't a limitation. It's the product's core trust mechanism and what makes it actually adoptable in an enterprise environment.

Core UX Principles

Principle 1

AI drafts, humans send

No output goes to any stakeholder without explicit PM approval. The "Send" action is always human-triggered. The AI can draft, stage, and suggest — nothing more. Non-negotiable.

Principle 2

Every answer has a source

All AI responses surface inline citations with clickable links to the source document. The PM can see exactly what the AI is drawing from. If a citation looks wrong, they can flag it immediately.

Principle 3

Uncertainty should be visible

The system shows a confidence score with every output. "High confidence" means 4+ corroborating sources. "Low confidence" means fewer than 2 — the PM should verify before using.

Three Surfaces, Three Trust Levels

// Interface Design by Persona
PM Workspace
Full conversational interface. Chat-style input, draft output with inline source citations, edit mode, one-click approve/send. Confidence score visible on every response. The PM feels in control throughout.
Lead Dashboard
Audit and oversight. Scrollable log of all AI actions across the team. What was generated, who approved it, what was sent, what was flagged. Confidence distribution chart. Quality trend over time. No ability to retroactively edit.
Stakeholder Portal
Read-only Q&A. Stakeholders can ask about roadmap status and get AI answers from approved, PM-gated content only. No access to internal docs. No edit. PM can review all stakeholder queries in their audit log.

MVP Scoping

What I'd actually
ship first.

One of the things I got wrong in my first draft of this case study: I designed the whole system. That's not PM thinking — that's engineering thinking. A PM asks: what's the smallest version of this that delivers real value and teaches us something important?

I applied MoSCoW to scope the MVP. The constraint: 8-week sprint, 2 engineers, 1 PM (me). No ML team. Must use existing LLM APIs.

MoSCoW Analysis

// MoSCoW — AI PM Co-pilot MVP
Must Have
Status Update Drafter — single integration (Jira only in MVP), generates a draft with cited sources, human approves before send. This is the 40% time-saver. Proves core value immediately.
Must Have
Decision History Q&A — PM can ask "why did we defer X?" and get an answer with a link to the source Slack thread or doc. Requires ingesting only Notion + Slack for MVP. No stakeholder-facing features yet.
Should Have
Audit log for PM Lead — simple dashboard showing all AI outputs, PM approvals, and flagged errors. Builds the trust story with leadership before you expand scope.
Could Have
Stakeholder Portal — valuable, but scope risk. Adds an IAM layer and new user type. Ship in V2 when core PM experience is validated.
Won't Have
Sprint review auto-generation, roadmap Q&A, multi-team support — real features, but premature. The MVP must prove that PMs will change their workflow. We don't know that yet.

The hardest call I had to make: the Stakeholder Portal was the most exciting feature from research. But shipping it first creates two user personas to support, doubles the IAM complexity, and we don't even know if PMs will adopt the core feature yet. Resist the urge to build the full vision in week one.

Phased Roadmap

MVP — Phase 1

Prove the Core Loop

Weeks 1–8 · 2 Engineers
  • Jira + Notion + Slack ingestion pipeline
  • Vector store setup (Pinecone, single org)
  • Status Update Drafter with citations
  • Decision History Q&A (text only)
  • PM audit log (basic)
  • Internal beta with 3–5 PMs
V2 — Phase 2

Expand & Validate Trust

Weeks 9–16 · 3 Engineers
  • Stakeholder Portal (read-only Q&A)
  • Role-based permission gating at vector level
  • PM Lead dashboard with quality metrics
  • Feedback loop for flagged outputs
  • Multi-integration support (Linear, Confluence)
  • First paying beta customers
V3+ — Future

Scale & Differentiate

Months 5–12
  • Sprint review auto-generation
  • Roadmap Q&A and feature tracking
  • Multi-team and multi-org support
  • API for existing PM tool integrations
  • Confidence calibration / hallucination detection
  • Custom LLM prompt library per org

09
What I Learned

What building this
actually taught me.

This case study started as a half-baked idea from watching a PM copy-paste context across five Slack channels. Turning it into a full product strategy forced me to be honest about what I didn't know — and figure it out.

01
Trust is a product feature, not a disclaimer
I initially thought the trust problem in enterprise AI was a communications challenge — just explain how it works and users will feel good. Wrong. Trust in B2B AI is earned through mechanisms: visible citations, editable outputs, human gates before any external send, audit trails accessible to leadership. Every trust mechanism is a design decision as intentional as the core UX.
02
My first scope was 4x too big
My original doc had 12 features in the MVP. After applying MoSCoW honestly, I cut it to 2. That forced a much sharper question: what is the single thing that proves this is worth adopting? Status Update Drafter. Everything else is infrastructure or delight — not proof. I think about this now every time I'm tempted to add "one more thing."
03
Architecture decisions are PM decisions
Choosing RAG over fine-tuning isn't just a technical call — it determines your accuracy maintenance costs, your data privacy posture, whether answers are explainable, and how stale your model goes when the roadmap changes. As a CS-background PM, understanding these tradeoffs at a conceptual level (not an implementation level) made my product strategy significantly sharper. I learned to ask: what does this architecture choice make easy or hard for users?
04
The "PM as editor" model unlocked adoption thinking
Framing the co-pilot as "AI that drafts, PM that edits" wasn't just a nice UX principle — it changed the entire adoption strategy. PMs who feared being replaced became PMs excited about getting their Monday mornings back. The product I designed around that one insight is more adoptable, more trustworthy, and more honest about what AI is actually good at.
05
Research found the product I didn't know I was building
I went into the interviews expecting to validate that "AI for PMs = automation." What I found was that PMs don't want automation — they want amplification. That's a completely different product. The interviews saved me from building the wrong thing entirely. I now understand why "start with research" isn't a platitude — it's the only way to build something people actually want to use.
